MTP Scripts and Causal Mechanisms

A Faculty Brief for DiMER-Aligned Teaching

The Claim

Medication Therapy Problems are the pharmacist’s functional equivalent of the physician’s diagnosis.

Just as physicians identify and name disease states, pharmacists identify and name failures or risks in the medication–patient system: wrong indication, ineffective therapy, unsafe therapy, and nonadherence.

This is not a loose metaphor; it maps directly onto one of the most well-validated models of diagnostic expertise development in health professions education.

The Schmidt & Boshuizen Model of Diagnostic Expertise

Henk Schmidt and Henny Boshuizen described a predictable sequence in the development of medical diagnostic expertise.

Progression of expertise
  • Novices reason with detailed, unorganized biomedical knowledge, making their reasoning slow, effortful, and often inaccurate.
  • Intermediate learners undergo knowledge encapsulation, compressing detailed mechanisms into higher-level clinical concepts.
  • Experts use illness scripts: mental templates organized around enabling conditions, a fault, and consequences.

Experts activate these scripts rapidly by matching patient cues to stored patterns, and when cases are atypical they can “unpack” encapsulated mechanisms and reason deliberately.

The Translation: MTP Scripts

If MTPs are the pharmacist’s diagnosis, then expert pharmacists develop MTP scripts with the same three-part structure as physician illness scripts.

Component Physician illness script Pharmacist MTP script
Enabling conditions Patient demographics, risk factors, exposures Polypharmacy, renal impairment, cost barriers, caregiver burden, complex regimen
Fault Core pathophysiology (for example, insulin resistance) Specific MTP plus cause (for example, “dose too low due to failure to titrate”)
Consequences Pattern of findings (for example, elevated A1c, fatigue) Clinical pattern of medication failure (for example, uncontrolled A1c, recurrent exacerbations)

The Critical Implication: What Must Be Encapsulated?

For MTP labels to function as expert-level diagnoses rather than checklist categories, each label must carry an implicit causal model.

That requirement tells us what foundational science to prioritize in the curriculum.

Indication MTPs

  • Disease pathophysiology → which mechanisms must be targeted → natural history with versus without therapy.
  • Students must understand why a therapy is needed, not just that guidelines recommend it.

Effectiveness MTPs

  • Drug–target interaction → pharmacodynamic effect → clinical endpoint translation → dose–exposure–response relationships.
  • “Dose too low” must mean something mechanistic, not merely “below the guideline range.”

Safety MTPs

  • Mechanisms of toxicity and vulnerable phenotypes (renal, hepatic, pharmacogenomic).
  • How changes in exposure convert benefit into harm.

Adherence MTPs

  • How missed-dose patterns map to effective exposure and outcome risk, including PK/PD “forgiveness” versus “fragility.”
  • Behavioral and system-level determinants that shape adherence.

How DiMER, Bayesian Reasoning, and MDP Support MTP Scripts

Framework Role in MTP script development
DiMER Builds the mechanistic “fault” substrate and structures what is broken and why, so that MTP labels such as “dose too low” or “needs additional therapy” rest on causal reasoning rather than rote classification.
Bayesian reasoning Makes MTP script activation explicit by formalizing how pharmacists move from vague concern to specific MTP: priors (which MTPs are common in this patient type?), discriminating cues (labs, refill data, side effects), and updated probability across competing MTP hypotheses.
MDP framing Extends the MTP “diagnosis” into a dynamic management plan where state = patient plus MTP profile, actions = dose adjustment, drug change, counseling, or device switch, and transitions = expected outcomes over time.

What This Is Not

  • Not replacing clinical judgment. The goal is to build toward expert-level pattern recognition, not away from it.
  • Not requiring formal Bayesian math at the bedside. We teach the logic of probabilistic updating, not the calculations.
  • Not overcomplicating simple cases. These frameworks scaffold novice reasoning; experts will eventually run MTP scripts automatically, just as physicians run illness scripts.

The Ask for Teaching

Teach foundational science as causal stories about indication, effectiveness, safety, and adherence, rather than as decontextualized detail.

Every mechanism we teach should connect to an MTP-relevant lever that students will later use at the bedside.

When “dose too low” carries an entire causal network—disease mechanism, drug mechanism, patient context, and expected trajectory—it becomes an encapsulated expert script. Without that, it remains a checkbox.
