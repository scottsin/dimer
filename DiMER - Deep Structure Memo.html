Foundational Year & DiMER

How the Technical DiMER Framework Supports the Infographics

Positioning Bayesian reasoning, MDP planning, and epistemic development as the deep structure beneath our faculty-facing infographics.

Abstract. The faculty infographics present DiMER as a simple, shared reasoning structure that keeps existing foundational science content intact while making it more usable for pharmacist decision-making. This memo shows how a deeper technical framework—integrating DiMER with Bayesian reasoning, Markov decision processes, deliberate practice, epistemic competence, and professional identity—underpins those infographics. Leaders can use the infographics to build broad faculty buy-in, while relying on the technical framework to guide curricular design, assessment, and scholarship.

The faculty infographics on organizing foundational sciences around pharmacotherapeutic reasoning are intentionally compact and practice-facing. They highlight the DiMER sequence (Disease → Mechanism → Exposure → Response), the novice-to-expert trajectory, and the link to PPCP and MTP identification without asking colleagues to engage deeply with cognitive science or formal reasoning models.

The more technical framework we have developed—integrating DiMER with Bayesian reasoning, Markov decision processes (MDP), deliberate practice, epistemic competence, and professional identity—sits beneath those infographics as the underlying architecture. This memo makes that connection explicit so that we can use both sets of materials coherently: the infographics to build initial buy-in, and the technical framework to guide design decisions, assessment strategies, and long-term curricular development.

1. What the Faculty Infographics Are Doing on the Surface

The infographics make three core moves that resonate with faculty concerns in the foundational year.

  • They reframe the problem: the issue is not “too much” or “too little” science, but a missing reasoning structure that connects disease biology, drug mechanism, exposure, and patient response in ways that support pharmacotherapeutic decisions.
  • They offer DiMER as a shared, repeatable causal sequence that keeps existing disciplinary content intact while making cross-course connections and pharmacist reasoning more explicit and usable.
  • They reassure colleagues that contact hours, disciplinary rigor, and course ownership remain stable, while the integration happens through shared cases, common language, and coordinated weekly structure.

In other words, the infographics describe what changes at the course and week level and how this helps students begin to think more like pharmacists, without requiring colleagues to adopt new formal reasoning vocabularies.

2. The Technical Framework as Deep Structure

Behind this accessible surface sits a more detailed framework that clarifies how DiMER, PPCP, MTPs, and expert development fit together. We have organized that deeper structure into five components.

  • Pharmacist-contextualized knowledge. DiMER provides the selection filter: foundational science is “core” when it helps students reason about Disease, Mechanism, Exposure, or Response in ways that are relevant to medication therapy evaluation.
  • Reasoning frameworks. DiMER specifies what to reason about; Bayesian logic describes how learners update probabilities across competing MTP hypotheses; MDP formalizes how they plan over time under uncertainty.
  • Deliberate practice and encapsulation. Repeated DiMER-based case work across PQA-aligned MTP categories supports knowledge encapsulation and the formation of illness, medication, and MTP scripts over time.
  • Epistemic and metacognitive competence. Explicit attention to uncertainty, confidence, and “what would change your mind?” prompts helps students learn when to shift between fast, pattern-based reasoning and slower, mechanistic reasoning.
  • Professional identity and adaptive expertise. Framing MTP identification and resolution as the defining professional act of the pharmacist connects repeated DiMER practice within PPCP to the development of a pharmacist identity and adaptive expertise.

The faculty infographics implicitly rest on these five components; the technical framework simply makes the cognitive and developmental mechanisms explicit so that we can design and study the curriculum in a principled way.

3. How the Pieces Fit Together

One way to see the relationship is to treat the infographics as our shared front stage and the technical framework as the backstage script we use when making design decisions.

  • When colleagues ask about weekly structure, shared cases, or alignment with PPCP and existing syllabi, the infographics provide an efficient, low-friction answer grounded in DiMER and MTPs.
  • When we are deciding which mechanisms to emphasize, how to scaffold case complexity, or how to assess reasoning and epistemic growth, the Bayesian, MDP, and deliberate practice components give us sharper tools.
  • When we study outcomes or write about this work, the technical framework allows us to connect our local implementation to broader literatures in cognitive psychology, diagnostic reasoning, and professional identity formation.

Using both layers together means faculty never have to “buy into” Bayes or MDP formalism to participate; they only need to see that DiMER makes their science more usable for pharmacist reasoning, while the technical framework helps the design team ensure that these changes are coherent, assessable, and theoretically grounded.

4. Practical Guidance for Use

In practice, we can position the materials this way.

  • For hesitant or overloaded faculty. Start with the one-pager infographics: they show that we are not reinventing the wheel, they anchor changes in DiMER and PPCP, and they emphasize manageability and student benefit.
  • For course leads and assessment leads. Use the technical framework to inform decisions about which content is “core,” how to structure cases over time, and how to assess reasoning, not just recall.
  • For internal scholarship and external sharing. Draw on the more formal components—Bayesian reasoning, MDP planning, deliberate practice, and epistemic development—when writing about why this model should generalize beyond our local context.

Framed this way, the faculty infographics and the technical framework are not competing narratives; they are two views of the same design—one optimized for clarity and feasibility in busy teaching lives, the other for coherence, rigor, and long-term curricular evolution.

Key takeaway

DiMER is the common thread. The infographics show how it improves alignment and student reasoning at the course level, while the technical framework shows how DiMER, Bayes, MDP, and professional identity development work together to produce pharmacists who can explain, predict, and refine medication therapy decisions across patient encounters.

This memo is intended as an internal framing tool for curriculum leads and faculty champions. The faculty infographics remain the primary documents for broad faculty distribution.
