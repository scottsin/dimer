Script Types in Pharmacist Clinical Reasoning

A DiMER-aligned faculty cheat sheet

This quick reference shows how illness scripts, medication scripts, and MTP scripts form a nested family of expert reasoning patterns, all organized by DiMER’s causal structure.

The Three Script Types

Script type What it explains Three-part structure Example How DiMER organizes it
Illness script What disease is occurring and why. Enabling conditions: Patient demographics, risk factors, exposures.
Fault: Core pathophysiology (what is mechanistically broken?).
Consequences: Pattern of signs, symptoms, lab findings. 		Type 2 diabetes
• Enabling: age >45, BMI >30, family history, sedentary lifestyle.
• Fault: insulin resistance plus β-cell dysfunction.
• Consequences: polyuria, polydipsia, elevated fasting glucose, acanthosis nigricans. 		DiMER Disease and Mechanism domains provide the fault and consequences structure for illness scripts.
Medication script How a regimen is expected to work (and fail) in a given patient type. Enabling conditions: Typical indication, patient phenotype, usual regimen.
Fault: How the drug mechanism addresses the disease mechanism.
Consequences: Expected therapeutic and adverse responses. 		Metformin in T2DM
• Enabling: T2DM with insulin resistance, adequate renal function.
• Fault: AMPK activation → reduced hepatic glucose output.
• Consequences: lower fasting glucose, early GI side effects, rare lactic acidosis risk. 		DiMER Mechanism→Exposure→Response chain structures how the regimen is expected to work and where it can fail.
MTP script What is going wrong with therapy and why. Enabling conditions: Polypharmacy, barriers, complexity, vulnerable phenotypes.
Fault: Specific MTP type plus mechanistic or systems cause.
Consequences: Clinical pattern of medication failure. 		“Dose too low” in HFrEF
• Enabling: up-titration not prioritized, fragmented care, hypotension concerns.
• Fault: neurohormonal mechanisms under-treated, inadequate receptor occupancy.
• Consequences: persistent symptoms, recurrent hospitalizations, unchanged biomarkers. 		Each MTP category maps to a break in the DiMER causal chain (see table below).

The Core Insight for Teaching

Expert pharmacists do not think in lists of drug facts. They activate scripts.

When an expert sees a new HFrEF patient, they instantly pull up an illness script for HFrEF, medication scripts for guideline-directed therapies, and MTP scripts for common failures such as “dose too low due to under-titration” or “nonadherence due to polypharmacy burden.”

These scripts are not separate knowledge stores; they share a common causal backbone: Disease → Mechanism → Exposure → Response.

Educational challenge How DiMER addresses it
Students learn drug facts but cannot apply them. DiMER ensures every drug is taught as a Mechanism that addresses a Disease fault, requiring sufficient Exposure to produce Response.
Students cannot explain why therapy failed. MTP scripts organized by DiMER make failures explainable: “The Disease–Mechanism link was wrong” or “Exposure did not match the plan.”
Knowledge does not transfer to new cases. Causal DiMER structure, rather than topic proximity, produces durable, transferable knowledge that supports far transfer.
Scripts form slowly or incompletely. Repeated case-based reasoning through DiMER in the first semester starts script formation early, with mechanistically robust fault components.

Bayesian Reasoning and MDP: How Scripts Behave in Time

Bayesian reasoning: Activating and revising scripts as data arrive

  • Question frame: “Given this new information (labs, refill data, patient report), which script—illness, medication, or MTP—just became more or less likely?”
  • Example: “Low potassium plus new muscle weakness increases confidence in a ‘loop diuretic dose too high’ MTP script versus a ‘disease worsening’ illness script.”

MDP (trajectory thinking): Planning sequential decisions

  • Question frame: “If I take this action now (titrate, switch, add device training), what state will this patient be in at the next visit or next month?”
  • Example: “If I step up ICS–LABA and address technique now, I expect better FEV₁ and fewer rescue inhaler uses in 4–6 weeks; if I delay, I may see an exacerbation by fall.”
