DiMER: Table of Student Transformations

Literature-grounded cognitive shifts that DiMER is designed to support

Working faculty reference • Based on literature-grounded synthesis

This table summarizes the major cognitive transformations we want students to undergo as they work with DiMER (Disease → Mechanism → Exposure → Response) across PHCY 502, 503, and 512.

Each row links a DiMER-framed transformation to underlying constructs in the education literature, what actually changes inside the learner, and key references that support the design choices.

Student Transformations Aligned with DiMER

# Transformation (DiMER framing) Technical construct / name What changes in the learner Illustrative references
1 From scattered facts → rich, layered D→M→E→R causal networks Causal mental models; structured causal networks; basic–clinical integration Students move from memorizing isolated biomedical “chunks” to building multi-level causal chains that link disease mechanisms, drug actions, exposure conditions, and clinical responses across scales (molecule → patient). Chi on experts organizing knowledge by deep causal structure; work on basic–clinical science integration; DiMER framework description.
2 From local links → runnable mechanistic subgraphs (for each DiMER link) Mechanistic reasoning; entity–activity frameworks Students can mentally “run” small causal submodels forward and backward (for example, receptor perturbation → pathway → organ effect → symptom, or inferring upstream mechanisms from a lab change), supporting therapeutic, target-engagement, and exposure–response hypotheses. Mechanistic reasoning in science education (entities, activities, multi-level mechanisms); DiMER’s emphasis on Mechanism and Exposure as causal links.
3 From atomized knowledge → encapsulated knowledge and early scripts Knowledge encapsulation; illness/therapy scripts With repeated case use, detailed biomedical knowledge is encapsulated into higher-order concepts (for example, “eosinophilic asthma,” “ICS–LABA regimen”) that still carry embedded mechanistic meaning and can be rapidly retrieved and unpacked, forming early illness and therapy scripts. Schmidt & Boshuizen on knowledge encapsulation and illness script formation; script theory and clinical reasoning literature.
4 From generic recall → proceduralized reasoning moves along D→M→E→R Proceduralization; reasoning frameworks; deliberate practice Students learn and practice explicit cognitive procedures (for example, “state the disease mechanism,” “write a pharmacotherapeutic hypothesis,” “locate where D→M→E→R likely breaks in this MTP”) until they become semi-automatic, supporting speed and reliability under load. Deliberate practice and mastery learning in medical education; DiMER described as a reasoning framework layered with Bayesian and MDP thinking.
5 From separate courses → cross-discipline mappings on shared cases Basic–clinical integration; multiple representations Students build stable mappings between concepts learned in different courses (for example, a pathophysiologic phenotype ↔ target class ↔ product/delivery constraints ↔ expected response), using DiMER as a single organizing representation for all three disciplines. Evidence for integrated basic/clinical science education; DiMER 3×4 responsibility matrix and coordinated roles documentation.
6 From descriptive → counterfactual and conditional reasoning about therapy Counterfactual reasoning; hypothetical reasoning; Bayesian updating Students increasingly think in if–then–else terms (for example, “If our D→M hypothesis were right and exposure is adequate, we should see response R; if we do not, which link is wrong?”) and learn to revise their beliefs when observations conflict with predictions. Bayesian reasoning as belief revision in clinical decision-making; DiMER–Bayes–MDP connection emphasizing probability shifts and revisable hypotheses.
7 From single MTP labels → MTP scripts as failure-mode schemas Script refinement; negative exemplars; error schemas Over time, students develop structured MTP scripts that link common medication-therapy problems to characteristic breaks in the D→M→E→R chain (for example, wrong mechanism versus inadequate exposure versus unrealistic response expectation), supporting both recognition and analytic troubleshooting. Script theory’s account of script refinement through experience and error; DiMER–MTP link positioning MTPs as the defining act of the pharmacist.
8 From naïve certainty → more sophisticated epistemic cognition Epistemic cognition; epistemic humility; uncertainty tolerance Students begin to see mechanistic and clinical knowledge as probabilistic and revisable, become more comfortable with ambiguity, and learn to monitor “How sure am I?” and “What evidence would change my mind?” about each hypothesis along D→M→E→R. Reviews of epistemic cognition and uncertainty handling in medical education; Bayes-framed prompts for confidence and evidence in the DiMER framework.
9 From routine competence → early adaptive expertise orientation Adaptive expertise; conceptual–procedural balance Repeated exposure to varied cases and explicit mechanism-based hypotheses nudges students from routine pattern-following toward flexible adaptation—modifying scripts and DiMER chains when faced with atypical patients or novel therapies. Hatano & Inagaki on adaptive versus routine expertise; deliberate practice in varied, increasingly complex cases; DiMER–Bayes–MDP connection.

Full citation details for the abbreviated references above can be found in the DiMER literature summary and associated framework documents.

How to Use This Table

You can use this table as a design checklist when planning sessions, cases, and assessments: Which transformation(s) is this activity targeting, and how will students’ work with DiMER actually move them along that dimension?

It can also anchor faculty development conversations by connecting everyday teaching decisions (what to emphasize, what to cut) to specific, literature-grounded cognitive outcomes rather than to vague notions of “coverage.”
